PEM Pacing Risk Score

Bar height = combined exertion risk today · dashed line = fatigue recorded the following day

How to read this: Each bar shows combined exertion risk for that day — physical (darker, bottom: steps/activity) stacked with cognitive (lighter, top: work hours). Colour reflects total risk: green = low (<2.3), amber = moderate (2.3–4.4), red = high (>4.4). The dashed red line is shifted one day right — the dot sitting above each bar shows the fatigue recorded on the following day, so you can directly see whether a high-risk day was followed by a crash. For example, the high red bar on Feb 26 (4 consecutive active days + part office day) has a dot of 8 above it — Feb 27's fatigue, a sick day — confirming the crash. Similarly, Mar 10 (full office day with high cognitive load) has a dot of 5 above it, reflecting Mar 11's elevated fatigue. Hover any bar for the full breakdown.

Lag Correlation: Activity to Fatigue

How strongly does today's activity predict fatigue over the following 5 days? Darker bars are statistically significant.

Calories predict delayed fatigue better than steps. The calorie signal remains significant at day+1 (r = −0.31) and borderline at day+2 (r = −0.23), while steps fade after same-day. Both metrics flip slightly positive around day 4–5, consistent with a boom-bust recovery cycle of roughly that duration.

Next-Day Fatigue vs Today's Steps

Does higher activity today predict higher fatigue tomorrow?

The downward trend (r = −0.20) reflects borrowed energy: high-step days tend to be your better days, but are often followed by a fatigue bump. Wide spread reflects variable PEM delay of 1–3 days.

Post-Run Fatigue by Run Length

Average fatigue on days 1, 2 and 3 after runs of 1–4 consecutive active days

4-day runs produce the clearest crash. After 4 consecutive active days: day+1 avg fatigue 7.2, day+2: 6.8, day+3: 6.3 — sustained across 3 days. After 3-day runs fatigue is surprisingly low (possibly reflecting the compression socks period), but 4-day runs consistently trigger multi-day crashes. Consider 3 consecutive active days as a personal energy ceiling.

Physical & Cognitive Exertion vs Fatigue

Office hours weighted 1.3x vs WFH to account for commute & social load · hover for notes

Work hours show a positive link with next-day fatigue (r = +0.20) — opposite to the negative correlation seen with steps (r = −0.20). This reflects that on high-fatigue days you rest physically but may still work, while cognitive load independently contributes to your energy envelope. Days where both physical and cognitive exertion are high simultaneously (e.g. Mar 10, Mar 26, Apr 2, Apr 13, Apr 17, May 4) are worth watching closely as they represent your highest total load days. Office hours are weighted 1.3× WFH to reflect additional commute and social demands. Days with no work data are weekends/public holidays.
